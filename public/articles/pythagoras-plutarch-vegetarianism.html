Pythagoras and Plutarch: Philosophical Perspectives on Animals and Vegetarianism

Pythagoras and Plutarch, two influential philosophers of antiquity, shared a profound commitment to the ethical treatment of animals and the advocacy of vegetarianism. In this blog post, we embark on a journey through their philosophical ideas, exploring their compelling arguments against the consumption of animal products and the enduring influence they have exerted on our society. It’s crucial to establish from the outset that this article is not focused on nutrition or scientific proof. While modern nutritional science consistently confirms the viability and adequacy of a plant-based diet for achieving a high-performance lifestyle, our focus here is the historical discourse on consuming animal flesh.

Pythagoras: The Transmigration of Souls and Animal Equality

Pythagoras believed in the concept of the transmigration of souls, which suggests that animals and humans share a common essence. According to this belief, animals possess souls that can be reincarnated into human bodies, emphasizing the interconnectedness of all living beings. This idea led Pythagoras to view animals as equals to humans, deserving of the same respect and consideration. Pythagoras advocated for animal rights, arguing that all living beings should be treated with kindness and compassion. He believed that consuming animal products went against the principle of non-violence towards all living beings. Pythagoras saw the act of killing animals for food as a form of violence and believed that it disrupted the natural order of life.

Plutarch: Animals’ Capacity for Pain and Ethical Consideration

Plutarch, another philosopher who shared Pythagoras’ views, believed that animals have the ability to feel pain and should not be harmed or killed for food. He argued that it was morally wrong to cause unnecessary suffering to animals for the sake of human consumption. Plutarch adopted a vegetarian diet himself and encouraged others to do the same. He believed that the human digestive system was not designed to handle flesh efficiently and lacked the natural attributes of carnivorous animals. Plutarch saw vegetarianism as a way to align with the natural order of things and live in harmony with the world around us.

Influence on Western Civilization

The ideas of Pythagoras and Plutarch have had a lasting impact on Western civilization’s understanding of animal rights and vegetarianism. Their advocacy for animal rights and vegetarianism has influenced subsequent philosophers, such as Plato and Seneca, who also aligned with these principles. The ongoing debate on vegetarianism and animal rights continues to be shaped by the voices of these ancient philosophers.

Pythagoras and Plutarch’s philosophical perspectives on animals and vegetarianism have left an indelible mark on Western civilization. Their belief in the ethical treatment of animals and the promotion of vegetarianism challenges us to reflect on our own consumption habits and consider the potential benefits of adopting a vegetarian lifestyle.
